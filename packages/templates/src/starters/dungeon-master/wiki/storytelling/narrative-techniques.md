# Narrative Techniques

> *"The dice tell you what happens. You tell the table what it means."*

Running a TTRPG is half rules referee, half storyteller. The rules handle the mechanics — who hits whom, how much damage, whether the door opens. But you handle the meaning — why the fight matters, what the door leads to, how the world responds to the characters' choices. This page covers techniques that make your game unforgettable.

## Show, Don't Tell

The cardinal rule of DMing. Never say "the room is dangerous." Say "the floor is slick with condensation, and you can see deep grooves carved into the stone where something heavy was dragged. The air smells of iron and something... acidic."

### Sensory Checklist

When describing a scene, hit at least three senses:

| Sense | Example Prompts |
|-------|----------------|
| **Sight** | What does the light look like? What's the dominant color? What movement catches the eye? |
| **Sound** | What's the ambient noise? What's conspicuously silent? What just stopped making noise? |
| **Smell** | What's the first thing that hits them when they enter? What's underneath that? |
| **Touch** | How does the air feel on their skin? Is the ground stable? What does the object feel like? |
| **Taste** | The metallic tang of blood. The acrid smoke of a fire. The stale water they just swallowed. |

### Practical Examples

**Bad:** "You enter the dungeon. There are goblins."

**Better:** "The tunnel opens into a wide chamber lit by sputtering torches. The walls are slick with moisture, and the stone under your boots is worn smooth by centuries of foot traffic. You hear chittering — high-pitched, excited — from somewhere deeper in. The smell hits you first: rotting meat and something sharper underneath. Goblins. At least a few, by the sound of it."

**Bad:** "The NPC is scared."

**Better:** "The mayor won't meet your eyes. His hands tremble as he pours wine he doesn't drink. When he finally speaks, his voice cracks on the second word. 'They took my daughter. Three nights ago. The guards... the guards found nothing.'"

## Pacing: The Heartbeat of the Game

Pacing is the difference between a session that flies by and one that drags. Think of your game's pace like a heartbeat: it needs to speed up and slow down.

### The Three Speeds

**Slow (exploration, roleplay, atmosphere)**
- Give details. Let players investigate. Describe the world.
- Let NPCs talk. Give them opinions, contradictions, secrets.
- This is where attachment forms. Players care about things they've spent time with.

**Medium (building tension, investigation, planning)**
- Information comes in fragments. Players piece things together.
- Stakes become clear. The deadline approaches. The villain makes a move.
- Questions outnumber answers. This is the "what do we do?" phase.

**Fast (combat, chase, crisis, revelation)**
- Short, punchy descriptions. No paragraphs of narration.
- Decisions have immediate consequences. No time to overthink.
- Raise your voice. Lean forward. The energy is infectious.

### Pacing Tools

**The Rule of Three:** If something important is happening, describe it in three beats:
1. Setup — what they see/hear/feel
2. Tension — something changes or raises the stakes
3. Resolution — what happens, what they must react to

**Cut on Action:** End a scene the moment a decision is required. "The door opens. Standing in the doorway is — okay, Alex, what do you do?" This keeps energy high and prevents over-description.

**The Breath:** After a tense moment, give the table a beat to react. Don't rush to fill the silence. Some of the best roleplay comes from those pauses.

## Managing Tension

### The Tension Curve

A good session follows an arc:

```
Tension
  |        Climax
  |       /    \
  |      /      \
  |     /        \  Resolution
  |    /          \___________
  |   /   Rising
  |  /    Action
  | /
  |/______________________________
         Time
```

- **Opening hook** (5 min): Start with action or a compelling question. Never start with "so, last time..."
- **Rising action** (60-90 min): Build through exploration, discovery, and escalation. Introduce complications.
- **Climax** (30-45 min): The big fight, the confrontation, the revelation. Peak energy.
- **Resolution** (15-20 min): Cool down. Loot, recovery, roleplay the aftermath. Set up the next session's hook.

### Cliffhangers

End sessions on a cliffhanger. The best ones are:

- A new piece of information that changes everything ("You open the letter. It's in your father's handwriting.")
- An immediate threat with no resolution ("The dragon's shadow passes over you. It's diving. Roll initiative.")
- A choice with no good answer ("The lever opens the gate, but it also floods the chamber where the prisoners are held. Pull it or not?")
- A question the players will argue about between sessions ("The villain removes their mask. You recognize the face. It's [NPC the party trusted].")

## Making NPCs Memorable

### Voice and Body

Players remember NPCs who feel different from each other. You don't need to be a voice actor:

| Technique | Effect | Example |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Pitch** | High or low voice | Gruff dwarf vs excitable gnome |
| **Pace** | Fast or slow speech | Nervous informant vs weary mentor |
| **Vocabulary** | Word choice patterns | Scholarly wizard vs street urchin |
| **Physical habit** | Recurring gesture | Stroking beard, tapping fingers, squinting |
| **Catchphrase** | Repeated expression | "By Moradin's hammer!" or "Interestin', interestin'..." |

One distinct feature is enough. Three is a character the players will remember for years.

### The NPC Triangle

Every important NPC needs three things:

1. **What they want** (motivation) — The innkeeper wants to pay off his debts. The guard captain wants recognition. The villain wants revenge.
2. **What they need** (subtext) — The innkeeper needs to feel like he's not a failure. The guard captain needs to believe the system works. The villain needs someone to understand why.
3. **What they're afraid of** (vulnerability) — The innkeeper fears his daughter discovering the truth. The guard captain fears he's been protecting the wrong side. The villain fears being forgotten.

### NPC Reaction Table

When players do something unexpected to an NPC and you need a quick reaction:

| d6 | Reaction |
|----|----------|
| 1 | Hostile — this NPC now has a grudge |
| 2 | Suspicious — they cooperate but watch carefully |
| 3 | Neutral — they respond proportionally |
| 4 | Curious — they're intrigued and want to know more |
| 5 | Helpful — they volunteer information or assistance |
| 6 | Invested — this interaction changes their goals |

## Player Agency

### The Unbreakable Rule

**Players must feel that their choices matter.** Every technique in this guide serves this principle. If the players feel railroaded — that their decisions don't change outcomes — the game dies.

### False Choices vs Real Choice

**False choice (avoid):** "The door on the left leads to the boss. The door on the right also leads to the boss."

**Real choice:** "The door on the left leads to the boss's chamber. The door on the right leads to the boss's armory, where you could sabotage their weapons before the fight. But you hear patrols coming, and you only have time for one."

### When Players Break Your Plans

They will. Every session. Your job is not to prevent this — it's to make their choices have consequences that feel real:

1. **Say yes, then...** Let them do the unexpected thing. Then figure out what happens because of it.
2. **Escalate, don't negate.** If they bypass an encounter with clever thinking, give them the win — but raise the stakes elsewhere.
3. **Let the world react.** If they kill the quest-giver, the quest-giver's family hires assassins. If they ignore the goblin raid, the village burns.

### Quantum Ogres

A "quantum ogre" is an encounter the party will face regardless of which path they choose. Use sparingly. When you do use one, the path the party chose should still matter — the ogre they face on the mountain path should be different from the one they'd have faced in the swamp, even if both are ogres.

## Description Techniques

### The Spotlight Rule

In any scene, each player should get a moment where their character is the focus. During exploration, ask each player what they're doing. In combat, narrate each hit and miss. In roleplay, make sure the quiet player gets asked directly.

### Evocative Language

Replace generic descriptions with specific ones:

| Generic | Evocative |
|---------|-----------|
| "It's dark" | "The darkness is thick enough that your torch only illuminates the first twenty feet — beyond that, nothing" |
| "The monster attacks" | "The creature surges forward, jaws wide, and you can see rows of teeth that have clearly been used before" |
| "You find treasure" | "Beneath the rotting cloth, something glints. Gold. A crown, bent and tarnished, with gems still set in it" |
| "The NPC is sad" | "Her eyes are red. She's been crying. She folds the letter and puts it in her pocket before she turns to face you" |
| "It's dangerous" | "You've seen death before, but this — this is wrong. The bodies aren't just killed. They're arranged" |

### The Callback

Reference earlier events in later descriptions. If the party saved a village in session 3, mention that village's rebuilt walls in session 12. If a character told a joke in a tavern, have an NPC repeat it in session 20. Callbacks make the world feel continuous and make players feel like their actions ripple outward.

## Managing Table Dynamics

### Reading the Room

Watch your players. Their body language tells you everything:

- **Leaning forward:** They're engaged. Keep going.
- **Checking phones:** Pace is too slow. Cut to action.
- **Talking over each other:** Energy is high. Channel it.
- **Quiet and hesitant:** They're confused or uncertain. Clarify the situation.
- **One player dominating:** Directly ask quieter players what they're doing.

### The Rule of Cool

If a player proposes something awesome that bends the rules slightly, let it happen — with a cost. "You want to swing from the chandelier, kick the goblin king off his throne, and grab his crown? That's incredible. Give me an Acrobatics check, DC 15. Fail and you land in the fire pit. Succeed and you're the new goblin king's worst nightmare."

The cost keeps it fair. The moment keeps it fun.

### Handling Rules Disputes

1. Make a ruling and move on. "Here's what I'm going to rule for now — we can look it up after the session."
2. Be consistent. If you ruled it one way this session, rule it the same way next session.
3. Admit when you're wrong. "I made a bad call last session. Going forward, here's how it works."
4. Never let a rules argument last more than 60 seconds during play. Nothing kills momentum faster.
