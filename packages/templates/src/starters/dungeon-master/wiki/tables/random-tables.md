# Random Tables

> *"Roll dice. Listen to the clatter. Something unexpected is about to happen."*

Random tables are the DM's secret weapon. They create moments you would never have planned, populate worlds with details you didn't anticipate, and take the campaign in directions nobody at the table expected — including you. Use them when you need inspiration, when players go off the map, or when you want the world to feel alive and unpredictable.

### Weather Effects on Gameplay

| Condition | Game Effect |
|-----------|-------------|
| Heavy rain | Disadvantage on Perception, extinguishes open flames, ranged attacks beyond 30 ft at disadvantage |
| Heavy wind | Ranged weapon attacks at disadvantage, fog dispersed, flying creatures must land or be blown away (DC 10 Strength) |
| Heavy snow | Travel at half speed, tracks covered in 1 hour, Perception checks reduced by 5 |
| Extreme heat (100+ F) | Con save DC 5 each hour or 1 level of exhaustion; heavy armor DC 10; water consumption doubled |
| Extreme cold (0 F or below) | Con save DC 5 each hour or 1 level of exhaustion; cold resistance grants advantage |
| Dense fog | Heavily obscured, blindsight/tremorsense valuable, advantage on Stealth |
| Thunderstorm | Lightning strike risk in open areas (tiny chance), deafened during thunder (1-2 rounds) |

## Using Random Tables Effectively

### When to Roll

- **When you're stuck.** The players did something unexpected and you need inspiration fast.
- **During travel.** Makes the journey feel like part of the adventure, not a loading screen.
- **To populate a sandbox.** Stock a hex crawl, a city district, or a dungeon level.
- **When the session needs energy.** A random event can reignite a flagging session.

### When NOT to Roll

- **When you already have a plan that's better.** Random results should inspire, not override good ideas.
- **When the result would derail the campaign's momentum.** If the party is racing to stop a ritual, a random merchant encounter slows things down.
- **When the result doesn't fit the tone.** Don't roll for quirky tavern events in a horror adventure.

### The Golden Rule

Roll the dice, read the result, and ask yourself: *"How does this connect to what the players care about?"* The best random results are the ones the players think you planned.
